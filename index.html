Social Media and the Web - Principles within "a contract for the web."

The points Eli Pariser makes in his TED talk were and still are incredibly important for a fair, free and accessible web. Even though the talk was released in 2011 we unfortunately still encounter the same systems and algorithms that are embedded in search engines and social media. Arguably it has become worse since then. With the more singular ownership over multiple social media platforms like Meta has with Facebook, Whatsapp etc... and Twitter being bought by Elon Musk there's more of a chance of people finding themselves lost in filter bubbles.

Both principle's 5 and 8 should be fundamental pillars of a free and accessible web. I believe that they go hand in hand with one another. A strong community should be built on trust and respect by both other users and companies. Our data is now a commodity that can be taken and sold when there should be a clear and accessible way of knowing what information you're giving up and who can access it. This lack of trust is a major issue between the user and large companies.

Principle 8 is lifted up with the help of principle 5. Trust is important for everyone to feel more safe online. Like the point mentioned in Pariser's talk there is a problem with accessibility and availability of the internet for some groups. There should be equal access to something that has become such an important tool in the modern day. To prevent access and availability to the internet is to cut someone off from a global online community.

Read / watch one of the items and respond below ...

I decided to read the Guardian article about the inventor of the first chatbot, Joseph Weizenbaum. After reading through the article i found it interesting that the first chatbot Eliza and modern day ChatGPT etc. are very similar to each other. Eliza was designed to emit an illusion of understanding so that it could 'pass' as a real person, similar to the chatbots we have today. But what exactly is ai? It was mentioned in the article how Eliza was designed with a set of rules to be able to return a plausible response and that something like ChatGPT is a large language model designed to mathematically predict what to say next. Both models essentially follow the same idea of having their own set of rules to determine an outcome. Essentially designed to trick the person using it into believing there is a kind of thought process rather than just information grabbing based on prediction.

The definition of intelligence from Oxford Languages is "The ability to acquire and apply knowledge and skills". I think that ai is intelligent in a different way to our definition or, at least, has certain aspects of the definition. AI has the ability to acquire knowledge from every corner of the internet and apply it to a response of a prompt given to it but doesn't have the ability to think, reason or truly understand what its being asked to do. It does things automatically when prompted. It doesn't truly think or analyze but but predicts. Mentioned in the article was what Weizenbaum said about "intelligence existing as a whole person, not just any one part" and that "intelligence isn't in the head but also the arm, wrist, hand." I think that ai today isn't truly/fully intelligent as it lacks some of the most important fundamental aspects of intelligence. Towards the end of the article Weizenbaum spoke about ai being capable of calculation but not judgement. A really interesting and i think important point was brought up about the idea that even if ai can do a task/ job, it shouldn't be allowed to do certain jobs like acting as a judge or a therapist. For as far as ai has come in the modern day i don't think it is 'actually intelligent'
